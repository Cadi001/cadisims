üìà Scenario 2: Stock Price Simulation

Setup: You're a financial analyst trying to understand potential future stock prices. Stock prices don't move predictably - they fluctuate randomly each day based on news, market sentiment, and countless factors. This simulation models these random daily changes.

Key Components: Starting stock price, daily volatility percentage (how much the stock typically moves up or down each day), time period for simulation, and random daily multipliers. Each day: New Price = Previous Price √ó (1 + random change within volatility range).

Real-world Application: Investment banks use this for option pricing (Black-Scholes model), portfolio risk management, regulatory stress testing, and helping clients understand investment risks and potential returns over time.

üé≤ Scenario 3: Dice Roll Analysis

Setup: You're designing a board game and need to understand the probability of different dice roll combinations. Rolling multiple dice creates different probability distributions - two dice won't give equal chances for all sums.

Key Components: Number of dice being rolled, number of trials to run, tracking frequency of each possible sum, comparing actual results to theoretical probabilities. For 2 dice: sum of 7 is most likely (6 ways to make it), while 2 and 12 are rarest (1 way each).

Real-world Application: Game design, casino odds calculation, quality control in manufacturing (testing if dice are fair), statistics education, and any scenario involving combining multiple random events.

ü™ô Scenario 4: Coin Flip Streak Analysis

Setup: You're analyzing whether a coin is fair by looking at streaks. Even with a fair coin, you'll occasionally get surprising long runs of heads or tails. This simulation helps understand what's 'normal' versus suspicious.

Key Components: Number of flips per trial, number of independent trials, tracking the longest streak of consecutive identical outcomes in each trial, statistical analysis of streak lengths. Even fair coins can produce streaks of 6-8 in a row fairly regularly.

Real-world Application: Fraud detection in gambling, quality control testing, sports analytics (hot streaks), market analysis (consecutive up/down days), and understanding randomness vs patterns in data.

üì¶ Scenario 1: Product Demand Simulation

üö∂ Scenario 2: Random Walk Problem

Setup: Imagine a drunk person leaving a bar, taking random steps either forward or backward along a straight line. This classic problem demonstrates how random processes behave over time and helps understand Brownian motion and market movements.

Key Components: Starting position (usually 0), step size (+1 or -1), number of steps to simulate, position tracking over time, and analysis of final displacement from starting point. Each coin flip or random number determines direction.

Real-world Application: Stock price modeling, particle physics, molecular movement, economic forecasting, and understanding how random events accumulate over time.

Click "Start Walking!" to start...

Real-world Application: Warehouse management, retail inventory control, supply chain optimization, understanding inventory policies, and cost trade-off analysis between holding inventory and stockouts.

üê∫ Scenario 6: Wolf-Sheep Predation Ecosystem

Setup: You're an ecological researcher studying predator-prey dynamics in a natural ecosystem. Wolves hunt sheep for survival, sheep graze on grass for energy, and grass regrows over time. This creates complex population dynamics that can lead to boom-bust cycles.

Real-world Application: Wildlife conservation planning, ecosystem management, understanding population dynamics, fisheries management, pest control strategies, and biodiversity preservation efforts.

Note: This opens a dedicated simulation environment with full ecosystem visualization, population charts, and detailed parameter controls including initial populations, energy gain rates, reproduction rates, and grass regrowth timing.
